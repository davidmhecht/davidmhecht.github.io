Research focus

I explore the intersection of lived religion, cultural knowledge systems, and species conservation in community-based protected areas. My work integrates anthropological and ecological approaches to understand how local spiritual practices, territorial deities, and place-based ontologies shape conservation outcomes and environmental stewardship.

Research interests

Thematic areas: Community-based conservation, anthropology of sacred landscapes, ethnobiology and traditional ecological knowledge, more-than-human ontologies, environmental anthropology, applied conservation ethics.

Methods: Participatory cultural mapping, collaborative ethnography, adaptive management, integrated conservation and development.

Geographic focus: Eastern Himalayas, with primary research in Bhutan and experience in East African conservation contexts.

Background

PhD in Integrative Conservation & Anthropology, University of Georgia (2022) | BA in Anthropology & Biology, Luther College (2011)

I combine disciplinary depth in sociocultural anthropology with integrated conservation science to develop innovative, culturally-grounded solutions to conservation challenges. My research prioritizes community partnerships, local knowledge, and the recognition of spiritual and relational dimensions of environmental stewardship.

Fieldwork & projects

Black-necked crane & white-bellied heron conservation in Bhutan

South-central Bhutan, high-altitude wetland communities

Long-term collaborative research examining how local protective deities (yul lha, gzhi bdag) and spiritual beliefs govern conservation practices and community stewardship in critical wintering habitats for endangered migratory species. Research involves participatory cultural mapping with wetland communities and the Royal Society for Protection of Nature (RSPN).

Partner: Royal Society for Protection of Nature (RSPN), Bhutan
Funder: National Geographic Society Young Explorers
Methods: Participatory mapping, ethnography, collaborative documentation
Impact: Informs adaptive protected area management and community conservation programs

Mapping more-than-human worlds of conservation in Bhutan

Kingdom of Bhutan, protected areas

Integrative cultural mapping project documenting local knowledge of Bhutanese landscapes and foregrounding ontological relationships between protective territorial deities and human communities. Uses collaborative methods and Himalayan Buddhist-inspired visual documentation to center lived religion in conservation planning.

Approach: Indigenous and Buddhist ontologies in conservation
Collaborator: Contemporary artist Gyempo Wangchuk
Funder: Firebird Foundation for Anthropological Research
Output: Dissertation research, conference presentations, digital documentation

Citadels of conservation: Protected species and protective deities in Bhutan

Eastern Himalayas, Bhutan

Doctoral research investigating the role of spiritual practices, protective deities, and sacred landscapes in shaping conservation policy and community participation. Examines how "citadels of the deity" — places imbued with spiritual significance and social prohibitions — influence protected area management and environmental integrity.

Key finding: Spiritual beliefs are operative forces in conservation outcomes
Application: Advances integrative conservation practice in Buddhist-majority contexts
Significance: Offers model for culturally-grounded protected area management
Status: Completed PhD dissertation (2022)
